Chapter 2:
How Anxiety Actually Works

Understanding what happens inside your body when you board a plane is the first crucial step toward taking control. To conquer fear, you must demystify it. Anxiety is not a sign of weakness; it is simply your body's survival mechanism misfiring in a safe environment.

When you step onto the jet bridge, your brain's fear center—the amygdala—might start scanning for threats. Because flying is not an everyday evolutionary activity, your brain interprets the new sounds, the enclosed space, and the sensation of movement as potential dangers.

Once the amygdala sounds the alarm, the adrenal glands spring into action. They release a sudden surge of adrenaline into your bloodstream. This is what causes your heart to pound, your breathing to become shallow, and your palms to sweat. Your body is preparing to fight or flee, even though you are securely seated.

Shortly after the adrenaline rush, your body releases cortisol, the primary stress hormone. Cortisol acts to maintain this heightened state of alertness. It keeps the "what if" thoughts looping in your mind, making it difficult to concentrate on a book or movie.

The good news? This chemical cocktail has a half-life. If you do not feed the anxiety with more catastrophic thoughts, the adrenaline dissipates within minutes. In the next section, we will explore practical grounding techniques to help flush these stress hormones from your system.
